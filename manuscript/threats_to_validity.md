## 8 Threats to Validity

### 8.1 Internal Validity

Several factors threaten whether the reported performance can be attributed to the confidence-aware multi-agent architecture.

**Calibration non-function and construct confounding.** The calibration pipeline produced only identity mappings across all folds, meaning fusion used raw, uncalibrated confidence estimates. The "no calibration" ablation produced Sharpe ratios numerically identical to the baseline (1.939 in both cases under the ablation protocol), consistent with this non-function. Any claim that the system operated with calibrated confidence is unsupported. The current experimental design does not isolate the contribution of the confidence module, making it impossible to determine whether it materially influenced performance under the evaluated conditions.

**Structural dependence between walk-forward folds.** The stride between successive folds (126 trading days) is substantially shorter than the training window (504 trading days), producing approximately 75% overlap between consecutive training windows. This creates serial dependence across fold-level observations not captured in the reported cross-fold variance estimates. The Kruskal-Wallis test confirms significant Sharpe ratio differences across folds (H = 17.86, p = 0.00047), but these may understate temporal non-stationarity because overlapping training windows smooth variation between adjacent folds.

**Indeterminate source of equal-weight agreement.** The close numerical agreement between CA-MARL and equal-weight across all folds admits at least three explanations: agents converge to uniform allocations independently; confidence-weighted fusion averages differentiated proposals into near-uniform weights; or the evaluation period's market regime is one where any long-only diversified strategy produces similar outcomes. The available data do not distinguish among these interpretations, and each implies a different conclusion about whether the multi-agent architecture contributes meaningful signal.

### 8.2 External Validity

**Single market and institutional context.** All experiments are conducted on nineteen Indian large-cap equities traded on the National Stock Exchange of India. Indian equity markets differ from developed markets in trading mechanisms (order-driven versus quote-driven), investor composition (higher retail participation), regulatory environment, and macroeconomic sensitivity. The Nifty 50 universe captures only large-cap, highly liquid stocks; results may not extend to mid-cap, small-cap, or illiquid securities within the same market.

**Temporal specificity of the evaluation period.** The data span January 2020 to June 2024, a period including the COVID-related market disruption and a sustained bull market. Performance varies dramatically across the four chronological folds (Sharpe ratios from 0.161 to 3.597), and the cross-fold variance component is approximately twenty times the cross-seed variance. This temporal dominance suggests any conclusion about system effectiveness may be period-specific. Whether the system would match or diverge from equal-weight in a bear market, range-bound market, or period with different volatility characteristics is untested.

**No out-of-sample evaluation on independent data.** The four-fold walk-forward design provides temporal cross-validation within a single 4.5-year period and asset universe. Although methodologically superior to a single train-test split, walk-forward validation within one dataset does not substitute for evaluation on a genuinely held-out market, time period, or asset class. Overlapping training windows further reduce independence between folds, so the four test windows provide less evidential separation than their count suggests.

**Constrained operational frequency.** The system operates on daily OHLCV data with daily rebalancing. All temporal parameters---the five-day label horizon, twenty-day reward stability window, and 504-day training window---are calibrated to daily data. Applicability to intraday, weekly, or monthly strategies has not been established, and the effect of rebalancing frequency on transaction costs and signal decay is not addressed.

### 8.3 Construct Validity

**Adequacy of the Sharpe ratio as the primary performance metric.** The Sharpe ratio captures the trade-off between mean excess return and its standard deviation, but does not account for skewness, kurtosis, or drawdown asymmetry. In portfolio contexts, these higher-order moments can materially affect investor welfare. The Sortino ratio and maximum drawdown are reported as secondary metrics but were not used for hypothesis testing. If the underlying return distributions exhibit negative skew or fat tails---common in equity portfolios---the Sharpe ratio may overstate risk-adjusted performance relative to a loss-averse investor's utility.

**Operationalisation of confidence.** Confidence is defined as a composite of three signals weighted by fixed, untuned parameters (historical accuracy 0.4, reward stability 0.3, prediction consistency 0.3). The historical accuracy component remained at an uninformative prior during the first fold, and the reward stability component showed limited variation across agents. Whether this particular formulation captures the theoretical construct of calibrated trustworthiness is not independently validated. Alternative formulations may operationalise confidence differently and yield materially different fusion outcomes.

**Incomparable calibration diagnostics across agents.** Outcome labels are generated on different scales depending on agent type: the market agent receives a continuous label (fraction of assets with correct directional prediction), while the risk and allocation agents receive binary labels (0 or 1). Expected Calibration Error and Brier score are sensitive to the information content and variance of the target variable. A continuous label permits finer-grained calibration assessment, while a binary label collapses the outcome space to two categories. Cross-agent comparisons of these diagnostics are therefore not interpretable as measures of relative calibration quality.

**Fidelity of the categorical-to-continuous proposal transformation.** The fusion module maps the market agent's categorical directional signals (BUY, SELL, HOLD) to continuous weight proposals via a deterministic mapping: BUY to +1, HOLD to 0, SELL to -1, with negative values clipped to zero and the vector renormalised. This discards magnitude information in the agent's recommendation and collapses negative signals uniformly to zero. The resulting proposal vector does not preserve relative strength of conviction across assets, and the proportion of SELL recommendations directly determines how much of the market agent's signal is retained after clipping.

### 8.4 Statistical Conclusion Validity

**Limited statistical power.** Five random seeds across four folds yield twenty fold-seed observations, but these are not fully independent: the same four equal-weight fold values are reused across all five seeds, and overlapping training windows create serial dependence across folds. The paired permutation test comparing CA-MARL to equal-weight produces a p-value of 0.3246 with a mean Sharpe difference of $-0.0455$; the sign test gives a win rate of 7/20 (p = 0.2632). Both tests are consistent with the null hypothesis of no difference, but with this sample size only large effects would be detectable. The observed Cohen's d of $-0.03$ is negligible, but its confidence interval is wide. A true effect of moderate size could exist and remain undetected.

**Imprecision of variance estimates.** The cross-seed standard deviation of the mean Sharpe ratio is estimated from five observations. The 95% confidence interval for the true standard deviation spans from approximately 0.045 to 0.412, meaning the observed value of 0.087 is compatible with substantially higher variability across random initialisations. The confidence interval for the mean Sharpe uses the normal approximation; a t-distribution with four degrees of freedom would yield a materially wider interval.

**Uncorrected multiple comparisons.** The pairwise Mann-Whitney comparisons across the four walk-forward folds are reported as significant at p < 0.01 without adjustment for the six simultaneous tests. The family-wise error rate under no correction is 5.8% at this per-test threshold, so at least one comparison may be significant by chance even if all folds were drawn from the same distribution.

**Absence of replication in the ablation study.** The seven ablation variants are each evaluated on a single temporal 80/20 split without replication across seeds, folds, or random initialisations. The CA-MARL baseline under this protocol (Sharpe 1.939) differs from the walk-forward cross-seed mean (1.885), confirming protocol-dependent results. Without error bars or replication, the observed differences between ablations (range: 1.842 to 2.010) are descriptive statistics rather than inferential evidence, and the ranking of variants---including the finding that removing the market agent improves performance---may not generalise beyond the single data partition used.
