## 7 Limitations

### 7.1 Implementation Limitations

**Confidence calibration does not produce non-identity mappings.** The calibration pipeline, which is designed to transform raw confidence scores onto a probability-calibrated scale using Platt scaling, outputs calibrated values that are identical to their raw inputs for every agent in every walk-forward fold. Runtime instrumentation confirms that the calibration fitting procedure never receives sufficient data to estimate a non-identity mapping. This is a deterministic consequence of the interaction between the walk-forward schedule and the temporal eligibility rule that prevents data leakage between folds: under the chosen schedule, the recommendation timestamps used for calibration accumulation consistently lie beyond the eligibility boundary. An alternative procedure that sources calibration pairs earlier in the temporal sequence was implemented but is not connected to the walk-forward loop. The calibration pipeline is structurally complete but inactive at the current configuration; alternative schedule configurations would be required for it to function.

The consequence is that the confidence scores consumed by the fusion module are uncalibrated raw estimates that carry no correction from the Platt scaling procedure. The reported calibration diagnostics (Expected Calibration Error and Brier score) therefore describe the miscalibration of the raw confidence estimates rather than post-calibration accuracy. The fusion module's confidence-weighted averaging operates on values that have not been probability-calibrated.

**The confidence estimation system has persistent internal inconsistencies.** Two distinct signals that feed into the raw confidence computation are degraded at source. First, the confidence value carried by every agent recommendation and used for calibration fitting is a hardcoded placeholder, while the system's concurrently computed raw confidence estimates differ substantially from this placeholder across the full range of observations. Even if the calibration pipeline received sufficient data, the fitting procedure would operate on placeholder values that do not reflect the computed confidence estimates. Second, the historical accuracy component of the confidence score---which carries the largest weight in the combination---never departs from its uninformative starting value across the entire experiment, despite outcome labels being generated. The effective confidence score therefore varies only through the two secondary signals (reward stability and prediction consistency), with the historical accuracy component contributing a constant term to the weighted sum. These two issues are additive: neither the calibration input values nor the historical accuracy signal provide differentiating information across agents.

### 7.2 Experimental Limitations

**Statistical power is constrained by the number of random seeds.** Five random seeds across four walk-forward folds produce twenty fold-seed observations. For the paired permutation test comparing CA-MARL to equal-weight, the mean Sharpe difference is $-0.0455$ (p = 0.3246). With five seeds, the confidence interval for the cross-seed standard deviation is wide: the observed value of 0.087 is consistent with true variability ranging from approximately 0.045 to 0.412. The reported confidence interval for the mean Sharpe uses the normal approximation; a $t$-distribution with four degrees of freedom would yield a materially wider interval. The twenty observations are not fully independent because each equal-weight fold value is reused across five seeds.

**The ablation study uses a different evaluation protocol.** All seven ablation variants are evaluated on a single temporal 80/20 train/test split rather than the four-fold walk-forward protocol. The CA-MARL baseline under this protocol (Sharpe 1.939) differs from the walk-forward cross-seed mean (1.885), confirming that results are protocol-dependent. Without replication across folds and seeds, the ablation findings---including the observation that removing the market agent improves Sharpe (2.010 versus 1.939) and that equal-weight fusion (1.951) and shuffled confidence (1.952) match or exceed the baseline---are descriptive rather than inferential.

**Transaction costs are not modelled.** All reported returns are gross of trading costs, slippage, and market impact. For a nineteen-asset portfolio with daily rebalancing, these frictions could materially reduce net returns. Transaction cost modelling is a recognised deferral.

**The static mean-variance baseline is adversely affected by the estimation window.** The sample covariance matrix estimated from 504 trading days for nineteen assets produces a negative Sharpe ratio ($-0.288$) and a maximum drawdown of $-18.8\%$. The large effect size comparing CA-MARL to mean-variance optimisation therefore reflects the instability of the static baseline rather than the strength of the proposed system.

### 7.3 External Validity Limitations

**Single geography and market structure.** All experiments use nineteen Indian large-cap equities (Nifty 50 constituents) traded on the National Stock Exchange of India. Indian equity markets differ from developed markets in trading mechanisms, investor composition, regulatory environment, and macroeconomic sensitivity. Results may not generalise to other geographies.

**Single time period with favourable market conditions.** The evaluation period (January 2020 to June 2024) spans the COVID-19 recovery and a sustained bull market in Indian equities. Performance is dominated by temporal market conditions: fold-level Sharpe ratios range from 0.161 to 3.597, the Kruskal-Wallis test confirms different Sharpe distributions across folds (H = 17.86, p = 0.00047), and the cross-fold standard deviation is approximately twenty times the cross-seed standard deviation. The close numerical agreement with the equal-weight baseline across all folds suggests that diversified long-only exposure was broadly rewarded during this period. Whether the system would differentiate from naive diversification in a bear market, a range-bound market, or a period with different volatility characteristics is untested.

**No held-out evaluation on an independent sample.** The four walk-forward folds provide temporal cross-validation within a single 4.5-year period and a single nineteen-asset universe. Performance on different time periods, asset classes, or market capitalisation segments is not evaluated. While the walk-forward design is methodologically superior to a single train/test split, it does not substitute for evaluation on data from a different market or epoch.

**Daily frequency.** The system operates on daily OHLCV data with daily rebalancing. The five-day label horizon, twenty-day reward stability window, and 504-day training window are calibrated to this frequency. Applicability to strategies at weekly, monthly, or intraday horizons is not established.
